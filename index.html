Wednesday

PULL + CORE
Wednesday focuses on the pulling muscles you use constantly in climbing, plus the antagonist pushes that keep your shoulders healthy. Superset = do A then B back to back with no rest, rest 90s between rounds.
SUPERSET 1 — 3 ROUNDS · rest 90s between rounds
💪
Pull-ups (or Lat Pulldown)
3 sets5–8 reps Bodyweight / ~50% stack No rest (superset) Primary climbing movement
▼
FORM CUES

Dead hang at the bottom — full extension every rep. Pull your shoulder blades down and together first (scapular retraction), then bend the elbows. Think "elbows to back pockets," not "chin to bar." Controlled descent — don't drop.

▶ YouTube: Pull-up form for beginners (Jeff Nippard)
STARTING WEIGHT / MODIFICATION
SituationWhat to do
Can do 5+ pull-upsDo bodyweight pull-ups, 5–8 reps per set
Can do 1–4 pull-upsUse assisted pull-up machine at ~30–40% of bodyweight assist (~65–85 lbs)
Can't do pull-ups yetLat pulldown machine: start at ~80–90 lbs (37–41% BW). Focus on full range of motion.
PROGRESSION

Once you can do 8+ reps with good form, either add 2.5–5 lbs (weight belt/vest) or reduce assist. For lat pulldown, add 5 lbs per week as long as form is clean.

🔄
Push-ups to failure
3 setsTo failure (aim 10–20) Bodyweight No rest (superset) Antagonist — shoulder health
▼
FORM CUES

Hands slightly wider than shoulder-width. Body forms a straight line — don't let hips sag or pike. Lower chest to 1 inch off floor, full extension at top. If regular push-ups are too easy, elevate feet. If too hard, drop knees.

▶ YouTube: Perfect push-up form (Athlean-X)
WHY THIS PAIRS WITH PULL-UPS

Climbing is almost entirely pulling. Without antagonist pressing work, you'll develop muscular imbalances that lead to shoulder impingement — the most common climbing injury. This is Alex Megos's #1 training tip: antagonist work.

SUPERSET 2 — 3 ROUNDS · rest 90s between rounds
🏋️
Single-arm Dumbbell Row
3 sets8–10 reps each side Start: 25–35 lbs No rest (superset) Lats, rhomboids, rear delts
▼
FORM CUES

Plant one hand and knee on a bench. Working arm hangs straight. Row the dumbbell toward your hip — think "elbow past your back pocket," not "lift to shoulder." Neutral spine throughout — don't rotate your torso to help. Slow on the way down (2–3 seconds).

▶ YouTube: Single-arm DB row form (Renaissance Periodization)

🔼
Tricep Dips or Dumbbell Overhead Press
3 sets8–10 reps BW dips / 20–30 lb DBs No rest (superset) Triceps, shoulders (antagonist)
▼
WHICH TO CHOOSE

If your gym has parallel bars or a dip station: do dips. If not, do seated dumbbell overhead press. Both work the antagonist pushing muscles. Dips are preferred for climbers as they also strengthen the shoulder stabilizers.

▶ YouTube: Tricep dip form (THENX)
STARTING WEIGHT

Dips: bodyweight. If too hard, use assisted dip machine. If too easy after a few weeks, add a dumbbell between thighs. Overhead press: start 20–25 lbs per hand. Focus on shoulder stability over heavy weight.

CORE CIRCUIT — 2 ROUNDS · minimal rest between exercises
🪝
Dead Hang
2 sets20–30 seconds Bodyweight Grip + finger tendons + lat stretch
▼
FORM CUES

Full grip on a pull-up bar. Arms straight. Shoulders relaxed and packed down (not shrugging). Breathe. Focus on feeling the stretch through your lats and opening your chest. This passively builds grip strength and finger tendon resilience — critical for climbing longevity.

IMPORTANT NOTE

This is NOT hangboard training. A hangboard applies specific finger positions with significant load. Dead hangs on a pull-up bar are safe for all levels and specifically build the connective tissue that protects against the most common climbing finger injuries.

🦵
Hanging Leg Raises
2 sets8–10 reps Bodyweight Lower abs + hip flexors
▼
FORM CUES

Hang from pull-up bar, arms straight. Slowly raise legs straight until parallel to floor (or as high as you can with control). Don't swing — use your abs, not momentum. Slow on the way down. If straight legs are too hard, bend knees and raise knees to chest instead.

▶ YouTube: Hanging leg raises form (FitnessFAQs)
CLIMBING CONNECTION

Essential for overhanging routes. Every time you bring a high foot up or maintain tension through a roof section, this is the movement pattern you're training.

🫙
Hollow Body Hold
2 sets20–30 seconds Bodyweight Full core tension — most climbing-specific
▼
FORM CUES

Lie on your back. Press your lower back into the floor — there should be NO gap. Arms overhead, legs straight and raised slightly (or bent for an easier version). Shoulders raised slightly. Hold this position with full-body tension. If your lower back lifts off the floor, raise your legs higher or bend your knees.

▶ YouTube: Hollow body hold tutorial (GMB Fitness)
WHY THIS IS THE BEST CLIMBING CORE EXERCISE

The hollow body position is exactly the body tension you need on steep terrain. Gymnasts and advanced climbers use this as a foundation. 20 seconds of perfect hollow body is worth more than 60 seconds of crunches for climbing performance.

Friday

ANTAGONIST + LEGS
Friday focuses on two things that most climbers neglect: shoulder health (antagonist) and leg strength. Strong legs mean you can stand on high feet and generate power from below rather than pulling everything with your arms. This directly enables harder grades.
SUPERSET 1 — 3 ROUNDS · rest 90s between rounds
🫁
Scapular Pull-ups
3 sets8–12 reps Bodyweight No rest (superset) Shoulder health — non-negotiable
▼
FORM CUES

Hang from pull-up bar with dead-straight arms. WITHOUT bending your elbows at all, pull your shoulder blades down and together — as if trying to tuck them into your back pockets. Your body will only rise 1–2 inches. Hold 1 second at the top, then slowly release back to a full hang. The movement is entirely in your shoulder blades, not your arms.

▶ YouTube: Scapular pull-ups tutorial (Climb Strong)
WHY THIS IS CRITICAL FOR CLIMBERS

Scapular instability is the root cause of most shoulder impingement and rotator cuff injuries in climbing. This exercise directly trains the muscles (lower trapezius, rhomboids, serratus anterior) that stabilize your shoulder blade under load. Do not skip this even when you're short on time.

↔️
Band Pull-Aparts
3 sets15 reps Light resistance band No rest (superset) Rotator cuff + rear delts
▼
FORM CUES

Hold resistance band at shoulder height, arms straight in front of you. Pull the band apart by squeezing your shoulder blades together — arms go out to your sides like a T. Hold 1 second at full stretch. Slowly return. Keep arms at shoulder height throughout, don't let them drop. Use a band that allows 15 clean reps without form breakdown — this is not a strength exercise, it's a motor control exercise.

▶ YouTube: Band pull-aparts for shoulder health (Alan Thrall)
IF YOUR GYM HAS NO BANDS

Do face pulls on the cable machine instead: set cable at eye level, pull toward your forehead, thumbs pointing back at finish. Same muscles, slightly different angle. Both are excellent.

SUPERSET 2 — 3 ROUNDS · rest 90s between rounds
🦵
Goblet Squat
3 sets10–12 reps Start: 25–35 lbs DB/KB No rest (superset) Quads, glutes, core
▼
FORM CUES

Hold a dumbbell or kettlebell vertically at chest height with both hands. Feet shoulder-width apart, toes slightly out. Squat down keeping your chest tall and elbows tracking between your knees. Go to parallel or below — don't cut it short. Drive through your whole foot on the way up. The weight in front helps you maintain an upright torso naturally.

▶ YouTube: Goblet squat tutorial (Alan Thrall)
STARTING WEIGHT
WeekWeight
Week 1–225 lbs — focus on full depth and form
Week 3–430–35 lbs
Week 5+40–50 lbs, add 5 lbs every 1–2 weeks
CLIMBING CONNECTION

Strong quads let you actually USE high feet instead of just hanging off your arms. Every time you see a climber stand up on a foothold smoothly, that's quad strength. This is where grades come from.

⚖️
Single-leg Romanian Deadlift
3 sets8 reps each leg Start: 15–20 lb DBs No rest (superset) Hamstrings, glutes, balance
▼
FORM CUES

Hold a dumbbell in each hand (or one in the opposite hand of your standing leg). Stand on one leg. Hinge forward at the hip — back leg rises behind you as your torso tips forward. Keep a flat back (not rounded) and the standing knee slightly bent, never locked. Lower until you feel a hamstring stretch, then drive your hip back up to standing. The movement should feel like a hinge, not a squat. Balance is the hard part — find a focal point on the floor to stare at.

▶ YouTube: Single-leg RDL tutorial (Jeff Nippard)
STARTING WEIGHT

Start at 15 lbs per hand — lighter than you think. This is a balance and motor control exercise as much as a strength one. Getting the movement pattern right is week 1's entire goal. Add weight (5 lbs) only when form is clean for all 8 reps.

WHY SINGLE-LEG OVER REGULAR DEADLIFTS

You almost never stand on two feet simultaneously while climbing. Single-leg work trains the stability and hip strength that transfers directly to footwork on the wall, especially smearing and standing on small edges.

CORE CIRCUIT — 2 ROUNDS
🚲
Bicycle Crunches
2 sets12–20 reps (each side) Bodyweight Obliques — twisting + balance moves
▼
FORM CUES

Lie on your back, hands lightly behind your head. Bring one knee toward your chest while rotating your opposite elbow toward it — genuinely rotating your torso, not just moving your elbows. Extend the other leg straight and low. Alternate sides in a smooth, controlled rhythm. Slow and controlled beats fast and sloppy every time.

▶ YouTube: Bicycle crunches proper form (Chloe Ting)
🗿
Plank
2 sets45–60 seconds Bodyweight Full core stability
▼
FORM CUES

Forearms on floor, elbows under shoulders. Body forms a straight line from head to heels. Squeeze everything — glutes, abs, quads. Don't let your hips sag or your butt rise. Breathe normally. If your form breaks before 45 seconds, drop to your knees and continue — bad form planks build nothing.

PROGRESSION

Once 60 seconds feels easy: try a plank with one foot raised, or a side plank (30 sec each side). Harder variation beats longer easier plank.

Progression Guide

THE GOLDEN RULE
Add weight only when you can complete all prescribed reps with clean form. Never add weight just because a week has passed. Form first, weight second — always.
RPE scale (Rate of Perceived Exertion): After completing a set, how many more reps could you have done? RPE 6 = 4+ more reps in the tank. RPE 7 = 3 reps left. RPE 8 = 2 reps left. RPE 9 = 1 rep left.

Weeks 1–4: Stay at RPE 6–7. Weeks 5–8: RPE 7–8. Week 9+: RPE 8–9 for main lifts.
EXPECTED PROGRESSION TIMELINE
WEEKS 1–2
Foundation
Everything feels easy and you'll still be sore. Don't add weight. Perfect your form on every movement.
WEEKS 3–4
Awakening
Soreness fades. Start increasing weights by 5 lbs where movements feel too easy.
WEEKS 5–8
Rapid gains
Muscle memory kicks in. Add 5 lbs weekly on rows and goblet squat. Pull-up reps increase fast.
WEEK 9+
Consolidate
Progress slows — this is normal. Reassess program, consider adding weighted pull-ups or heavier leg work.
🪝 Hangboard timeline: Do NOT add a hangboard until you're climbing consistent 5.11 with 1–2 years of recent climbing history and zero finger/elbow issues. Your tendons need years to catch up to your muscles. The dead hang in your current program is safe and sufficient for now. Revisit in 6–12 months.

Key Notes

💊 Post-workout nutrition: After climbing sessions (Mon, Thu, Sat), get protein within 30–60 min. Greek yogurt, protein shake, or eggs. After strength sessions (Wed, Fri), a regular protein-focused meal within 2 hours is sufficient.
😴 Sleep and gains: Your muscles and tendons don't get stronger during workouts — they get stronger during sleep. Your Whoop data will show higher HRV on mornings after good sleep following hard sessions. The night shower → podcast → sleep routine you're building is directly supporting your climbing improvement.
⚖️ Weight loss + strength training: At a calorie deficit (which you'll be in), you won't build much new muscle mass. That's fine. The goal here is to maintain and re-activate existing muscle, improve movement patterns, and build the specific strength that translates to climbing. You'll look and perform noticeably better without the scale moving dramatically.
🛑 When to stop a set: Form breakdown, sharp joint pain, or shaking that compromises the movement. Muscle burn and fatigue are fine. Wrist, elbow, or shoulder pain is not — stop, rest, and if it persists get it looked at. Climbing injuries are mostly overuse and most are preventable.
